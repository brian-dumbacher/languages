I. Cases


German has four grammatical cases:


  1. nominative (nom.) for subjects
  2. accusative (acc.) for direct objects
  3. dative (dat.) for indirect objects
  4. genitive (gen.) for expressing possession

II. Genders


German has three grammatical genders:


  1. masculine (M)
  2. feminine (F)
  3. neuter (N)

III. Numbers


German has two grammatical numbers:


  1. singular (sing.)
  2. plural (pl.)

IV. Nouns

V. Persons


German has three grammatical persons:


  1. 1st for referring to oneself
  2. 2nd for referring to the addressee
  3. 3rd for referring to someone outside the conversation

VIII. Moods


German has three grammatical moods:


  1. indicative (ind.) for stating facts (as the speaker sees them)
  2. subjunctive (sub.) for expressing hypothetical situations and conveying indirect discourse
  3. imperative (imp.) for giving commands

IX. Tenses


German has six grammatical tenses:


  1. present
  2. present perfect
  3. past
  4. past perfect
  5. future
  6. future perfect

X. Voices


German has two grammatical voices:


  1. active
  2. passive

XII. Prepositions


Prepositions can be classified in terms of the case of their object. "One-way" prepositions always take their object in a specific case (accusative, dative, or genitive). "Two-way" prepositions, on the other hand, take their object in either the accusative or dative case depending on the movement described. If the preposition describes movement from one place to another, the accusative case is used. If the preposition describes movement constrained to a single place or no movement at all, the dative case is used. Table 19 lists common one-way prepositions and the nine two-way prepositions.
